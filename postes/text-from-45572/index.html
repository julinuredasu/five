    A significant security concern related to communications from short codes like 45572 is the threat of smishing (SMS phishing) and impersonation. Malicious entities can employ sophisticated techniques to spoof sender IDs, making a fraudulent message appear to originate from a legitimate short code or a numerically similar identifier. Such messages often contain deceptive links designed to harvest credentials or install malware, or they may prompt recipients to call fraudulent numbers. The perceived authenticity of a short code source can lead recipients to trust these illegitimate messages, thereby compromising personal information or account security. Vigilance is required to differentiate genuine communications from sophisticated imitations, particularly when unsolicited links or requests for sensitive data are present.

  • Compromise of Delivery Mechanism (SIM Swapping)

    While the origin of messages from 45572 is typically secure, the delivery mechanism to the recipient's device can be subject to compromise, primarily through SIM swapping attacks. In such scenarios, attackers illegally port a user's phone number to a SIM card they control. This allows them to intercept all incoming SMS messages, including critical One-Time Passcodes (OTPs) and security alerts dispatched from 45572. If an attacker initiates a password reset or a high-value transaction, the essential authentication codes intended for the legitimate user are instead delivered to the attacker, facilitating complete account takeover. The reliance on SMS for multi-factor authentication, though robust against many threats, becomes a vulnerability when the underlying phone number is compromised.

Lack of awareness regarding phishing tactics or the proper use of authentication codes makes recipients susceptible to social engineering, allowing attackers to bypass legitimate security protocols through manipulation rather than technical breach.

Causes and Symptoms of Tuberculosis

TB is caused by the bacterium Mycobacterium tuberculosis, which is typically spread through the air when an infected person coughs, sneezes, or talks. The symptoms of TB can vary depending on the location and severity of the infection, but common symptoms include coughing, chest pain, fever, and weight loss. In some cases, TB can also affect other parts of the body, such as the brain, spine, or kidneys.

Diagnosis and Treatment of Tuberculosis

Diagnosing TB typically involves a combination of clinical evaluation, laboratory tests, and imaging studies. The most common laboratory test used to diagnose TB is the sputum smear microscopy, which involves examining a sample of sputum under a microscope for the presence of TB bacteria. If a diagnosis of TB is confirmed, treatment typically involves a combination of antibiotics, which are taken over a period of six months or more. In some cases, surgery may also be necessary to remove damaged tissue or repair damage to the lungs or other organs.

Recent Developments in Tuberculosis Control in Singapore

In recent years, Singapore has made significant progress in controlling TB, thanks to a combination of effective public health measures and advances in medical technology. One of the key developments has been the introduction of new diagnostic tests, such as the GeneXpert MTB/RIF assay, which can rapidly detect TB bacteria and identify resistance to rifampicin, a key antibiotic used to treat TB. Additionally, the MOH has launched initiatives to improve the detection and treatment of latent TB infection, which is a major risk factor for the development of active TB disease.

Projections for 2025

Looking ahead to 2025, the MOH has set ambitious targets for reducing the incidence of TB in Singapore. These include reducing the number of reported cases of TB to fewer than 1,000 per year, and achieving a treatment success rate of at least 90% for all patients with TB. To achieve these targets, the government plans to continue investing in public health measures, such as contact tracing and public awareness campaigns, as well as expanding access to new diagnostic tests and treatments.

Conclusion

In conclusion, TB remains a significant public health concern in Singapore, but the government has made significant progress in controlling the disease in recent years.
